Projects

My research is organised around two major book projects on armed politics, borderland governance, and digital illicit economies, alongside a research-led workshop series on methods, gender, and fieldwork in conflict settings.

Borderland Statecraft: Armed Politics in Southeast Asia’s Contested Borderlands
Book project, under contract with Cornell University Press
Growing out of my PhD at SOAS University of London (2020–2024), this book project examines how armed organisations such as the Wa and Kokang have navigated between the Myanmar state and China. It argues that China has shaped their trajectories not only as a neighbouring power, but also as a political model, an institutional influence, and a historical force. By tracing how non-state armed actors selectively adapted Chinese forms of party organisation, territorial administration, and governance, the project explains why political authority in the China–Myanmar borderlands has developed along divergent paths.

Related publications:
“The Politics of Persistence: China’s Border Government and Aid to the Communist Party of Burma, 1969–1989.” Asian Studies Review (2025).
“The Enduring Legacy and Historical Continuity of Kokang’s Mutinies in the China-Myanmar Borderlands.” Journal of Contemporary Asia (2025).
“Playing with Fire: How Engagement with Illicit Economies Shapes the Survival and Resilience of Ethnic Armed Organisations in the China-Myanmar Borderlands.” China Perspectives (2024).

Logistics Politics in the Cracks: Digital Illicit Economies at the Margins
Second book project
My second book project examines the online scam industry and the wider political economy of digital illicit economies across Southeast Asia and beyond. It explores how scam operations are embedded in borderland governance, logistical systems, and transnational infrastructures, and how they reshape relations between sovereignty, mobility, labour, and violence. More broadly, the project uses empirically grounded regional research to address wider International Relations questions about sovereignty, order, conflict, and power.

Selected outputs and ongoing work:
“The Logistics and (Im)Mobilities of Digital Illicit Economies: The Online Scam Industry in Southeast Asia.” Mobilities, forthcoming / special issue project.
“Illicit Economies as Political Leverage: Armed Group Strategies and the Politics of Recognition.” Article in progress.
Ongoing research on checkpoint systems and the comparative politics of control across Southeast Asia and West Africa.

Female Scholars in Conflict Studies
Convened workshop series, SOAS University of London, 2025–2026
I convene the Female Scholars in Conflict Studies workshop series, a research-led initiative that brings together discussions on methods, ethics, gender, and access in the study of conflict and hostile contexts. The series provides a platform for methodological reflection, intellectual exchange, and support for scholars working on difficult field sites and politically sensitive topics.

Selected workshops:

“Doing Ethnography in Hostile Contexts: A Conversation with Professor Ching Kwan Lee” (February 2026).
“Female Scholars in Conflict Studies: Research Methods Reflections” (July 2025).

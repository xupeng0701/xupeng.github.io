Research

Research Overview

As a political economist, my research examines how political and economic life is organised at the margins of the state and the market, with a particular focus on borderlands, illicit economies, and the transnational reach of Chinese capital. It is grounded in a long-standing engagement with borderlands as sites where sovereignty, mobility, and economic life are continuously negotiated. Over time, this broad interest developed into a more focused concern with the China–Myanmar borderland, where armed politics, illicit economies, and the transnational reach of Chinese capital converge in particularly concentrated form. From this foundation, my work has developed along three closely related lines of inquiry: the persistence of armed organisations in contested borderlands, the resilience and reorganisation of digital illicit economies, and the methodological and ethical challenges of conducting research in conflict and hostile contexts.

Research Themes

1. Armed Organisations, Borderland Authority, and Political Persistence

One major strand of my research examines how armed organisations endure over time and how they navigate relations between incumbent states and neighbouring powers. Focusing especially on the China–Myanmar borderland, this work asks how actors such as the Wa and Kokang have converted geopolitical in-betweenness into durable forms of political authority. It is particularly concerned with the institutional, territorial, and historical conditions under which non-state armed actors sustain governance, adapt external models, and consolidate long-term political order in contested borderlands.

This strand informs my first book project, Borderland Statecraft: Armed Politics in Southeast Asia’s Contested Borderlands.

2. Digital Illicit Economies, Logistics, and Resilience

A second strand of my research focuses on the transformation and persistence of illicit economies, especially digital illicit economies. Here I examine how online scam industries are embedded in wider systems of borderland governance, logistical organisation, labour control, and cross-border infrastructure. Rather than treating these economies as temporary criminal anomalies, this research approaches them as adaptive and durable formations that are able to relocate, reorganise, and remain embedded within broader regional and global political-economic structures.

This strand underpins my current three-year Manchester project and my second book project, Logistics Politics in the Cracks: Digital Illicit Economies at the Margins.

3. Methods, Gender, and Research in Hostile Contexts

A third strand of my research concerns the production of knowledge in conflict and hostile environments. This work reflects on questions of methodology, ethics, gender, and access, with particular attention to the challenges faced by women scholars conducting research in politically sensitive and difficult field settings. It asks how fieldwork is shaped by vulnerability, positionality, and unequal structures of access, and how methodological reflection can contribute to more rigorous and self-aware research on conflict settings.

This strand is also developed through the Female Scholars in Conflict Studies workshop series.

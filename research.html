Research

Research Overview

As a political scientist, my research examines how political and economic life is organised at the margins of the state and the market, with a particular focus on borderlands, illicit economies, and the transnational reach of Chinese capital. It is grounded in a long-standing engagement with borderlands as sites where sovereignty, mobility, and economic life are continuously negotiated. Over time, this broad interest developed into a more focused concern with the China–Myanmar borderland, where armed politics, illicit economies, and the transnational reach of Chinese capital converge in particularly concentrated form. From this foundation, my work has developed along three closely related lines of inquiry: borderland statecraft and armed politics, logistics politics and digital illicit economies, and the methodological and ethical challenges of conducting research in conflict and hostile contexts.

Research Themes

1. Borderland Statecraft and Armed Politics

One major strand of my research examines how armed organisations persist over time in contested borderlands and how they navigate relations between incumbent states and neighbouring powers. Focusing especially on the China–Myanmar borderland, this work analyses how actors such as the Wa and Kokang have converted geopolitical in-betweenness into durable political forms. It is concerned with the historical, territorial, and institutional conditions under which armed groups sustain themselves, adapt external models, and produce long-term political order in spaces of contested sovereignty.

2. Logistics Politics and Digital Illicit Economies

A second strand of my research examines the margins of market economies through the case of digital illicit economies, especially the online scam industry. Rather than approaching scam economies narrowly as criminal anomalies, this work analyses them as political-economic formations embedded in wider systems of logistics, (im)mobility, labour control, and cross-border infrastructure.

This research is particularly concerned with variation across regional settings. In Myanmar, digital illicit economies are closely embedded in conflict economies and war economies. In Cambodia, by contrast, they must also be understood in relation to neoliberal development, special economic zones, and speculative urbanisation. A further comparative dimension extends this inquiry to West Africa, where scam economies likewise illuminate broader questions about mobility, brokerage, and the political organisation of illicit markets.

3. Methods, Gender, and Research in Hostile Contexts

A third strand of my research concerns the production of knowledge in conflict and hostile environments. This work reflects on questions of methodology, ethics, gender, and access, with particular attention to the challenges faced by women scholars conducting research in politically sensitive and difficult field settings. It asks how fieldwork is shaped by vulnerability, positionality, and unequal structures of access, and how methodological reflection can contribute to more rigorous and self-aware research on conflict settings.
